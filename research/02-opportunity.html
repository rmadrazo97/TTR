The Recovery Opportunity

Three distinct tax pools, one shared ingestion pipeline. Together they total ~€6,000 per international truck per year — before driver income-tax relief.

2.1 Three stackable revenue streams

2.2 Stream A — Cross-border VAT refund (Directive 2008/9/EC, successor to the 8th Directive)

A carrier VAT-registered at home but not in the country where it bought diesel/tolls/repairs reclaims that foreign VAT electronically through its home portal — it does not file a foreign return.

  • Deadline: 30 September of the following year — hard, no extensions.
  • Thresholds (Art. 17): €400 for a 3–11 month period; €50 for a full year. Invoice copies required where base ≥ €1,000 — ≥ €250 for fuel (most fills clear this → shapes the ingestion product).
  • Timeline: refund state decides in 4 months (6–8 if it queries); interest owed on delay.
  • Non-EU carriers (UK, CH, TR) use the slower, paper-based 13th Directive.

Legal wrinkle — the fuel-card question (CJEU Vega International C-235/18)

When diesel is bought through a fuel card, the card issuer's supply can be treated as a VAT-exempt grant of credit — meaning the pump receipt is not the claimant's valid invoice and recoverability depends on the issuer's commissionaire structuring. The "photo of receipt" pipeline must know whose invoice it holds (station vs issuer). This is the single biggest legal risk to Stream A and a product-design constraint.

2.3 Stream B — Professional-diesel excise refunds

A partial refund of national diesel excise down to a "commercial diesel" floor. Universal eligibility: gross vehicle weight ≥ 7.5 t. Rates are volatile (quarterly/annual) and vary sharply by country — so the platform must treat them as live data.

No professional-diesel scheme in DE, NL, PL, AT, LU or Scandinavia → only VAT is recoverable there. Best case, a truck fuelling entirely in France (~35,000 L/yr) recovers €5,446 in excise; blended across real mixed-EU routes the fleet average is far lower (~€1,200/truck, see §3.2) — excise is upside, not the base case. Shrinking pool: Italy gates to Euro 5+ (excludes older tractors), France/Belgium rates are on a legislated downward path, and the EU Energy Taxation Directive recast may end commercial-diesel carve-outs.

2.4 Stream C — Per-diem & days-abroad income tax (driver-level)

Tax-free daily allowances for meals and in-cab overnights reduce the driver's taxable income — and, in Poland/Romania/Lithuania, the social-contribution base (a much bigger cash lever). It is a count-the-days problem: the proof already lives in tachograph/GPS data.

Worked example — Germany (employed driver)

A driver with ~220 travel days recovers ~€7,680 in meal + overnight allowances vs the €1,230 default lump sum → ~€1,600–1,900 cash refund per year, recurring. In PL/RO/LT the contribution-base reduction can be worth several thousand € more.

Critical — employee vs self-employed (autónomo)

The exemptions above apply to employed drivers (payroll). A self-employed autónomo — our Spanish beachhead — cannot pay himself an exempt dieta; he deducts only limited meal costs (ES: €26.67 domestic / €48.08 abroad, requiring electronic payment at a hospitality establishment — in-cab meals don't qualify). So for an autónomo-first pilot, Stream C is a payroll-structuring product for carriers that employ drivers, plus a modest autónomo deduction — not a large ex-post refund. Do not size the beachhead on it.

2.5 Why the money goes unclaimed — our wedge

  • Invoice-format rules: a card slip or simplified till receipt is rejected — needs full supplier name, address, VAT ID.
  • Multi-portal friction: every country is a different form, software, and language.
  • Cash-flow lag: VAT 4–8 months; some excise 6–18 months.
  • Thresholds & deadlines: below €400/€50 discourages the small operator; miss 30 Sep and it's gone.
  • Record-keeping: per-diem relief needs proof of days/overnights — data drivers rarely reconstruct.
  • Under-claiming is systemic — the entire specialist-agent industry exists on this gap.
Look-back — the first-engagement multiplier

A new customer can usually recover multiple prior years, not just the current one: French excise ~2 years back, Spanish IRPF rectificación 4 years, German income tax 4 years. First-year recovery per customer is often 2–4× the annual run-rate — a strong acquisition hook and a de-risker for the pilot's per-truck recovery target.
